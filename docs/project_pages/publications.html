Introduction

One of my favorite things to do is explore. I explore by walking around the city. I explore by finding new pathways through campus. I explore by solving new algorithms or math problems. But my favorite type of exploration is trying things that nobody has ever done before. Naturally, this implies that I really enjoy working on research questions! I'm currently working at Learning and Intelligent Systems (LIS) (Leslie Kaelbling and Tomas Lozano-Perez) and Biomechatronics (Hugh Herr). In LIS, I work on robotic manipulation using convolutional occupancy networks as autoencoders for point clouds. At Biomech, I build and test robotic ankles and knees to restore walking, getting up, and climbing stairs for trans-tibial and trans-femoral amputees.

The following are some interesting bits from my research at these labs, as well as links to my publications.

Local Neural Descriptor Fields: Locally Conditioned Object Representations for Manipulation

Here, we are developing a way to teach a robot how to manipulate objects based on very few demonstrations. Previous works can manipulate objects that are from the same object class as the demonstrations (e.g. mug demos --> mug manipulation); however they cannot generalize to new object classes (mug demos --> bowl with handle manipulation). Our thesis is that a local encoding of the shape would allow for better generalization. For example, grabbing a handle shouldn't be affected by what the handle is attached to.

The paper has just been submitted to ICRA 2023 so I can't quite link the pdf, but I'll update this at some point!

Modulation of Prosthetic Ankle Plantarflexion Through Direct Myoelectric Control of a Subject-Optimized Neuromuscular Model

The title is long, but basically we made a robot ankle that helps our subject stand on their tip toes (something that can't be done with any other system), among other tasks. I did a lot of the mechatronics and code implementation while my supervisor developed the muscle model that interprets our input electrode (EMG) signals. The paper is here!
